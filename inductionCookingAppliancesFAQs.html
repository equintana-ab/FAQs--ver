Frequently Asked Questions

Here are quick answers to questions shoppers ask the most.

Induction cookers are faster, safer, and more energy-efficient. They heat up quickly, have no open flame, and are ideal for homes—especially in small spaces or households with kids.

Induction Cooker: A portable countertop appliance that heats cookware directly using electromagnetic technology. It’s fast, energy-efficient, and safe since the surface stays cool. Ideal for small kitchens or single-burner cooking.

Induction Hob: A built-in flat cooking surface with one or more induction zones. Essentially, a type of induction cooker integrated into your countertop, perfect for modern kitchens with multiple burners.

Induction Stove: A standalone unit that may combine an induction cooktop with an oven. Offers versatility for both cooking and baking in one appliance.

Electric Oven: An enclosed appliance that heats food by circulating hot air, making it ideal for baking, roasting, or slow-cooking dishes. Unlike induction cookers or hobs, it doesn’t heat the pan directly.

  • Voltage: Most built-in induction cooktops require 220–240V power. Some smaller or portable units can run on 110–120V, but with limited performance.
  • Dedicated breaker: It’s recommended and often required. Typical range: 20A–40A breaker (depends on the model). It prevents overloading when cooking at high heat.
  • Dedicated Circuit: It should not share a circuit with other appliances (like microwaves or ovens). Sharing can cause frequent breaker trips.
  • Wiring: Induction cooktops usually require thicker gauge wiring: common range: 6–10 AWG (depends on load). Installed directly from the electrical panel to the cooktop.
  • Plug-in or hardwired: Most built-in induction cooktops are hardwired (no standard plug).
  • Some models allow a high-power 220V outlet, but still need a proper electrical setup.
  • Check if your unit supports 220V lines
  • Verify your electrical panel capacity (older buildings may be limited)
  • Coordinate with the building admin or maintenance before installation

Improper setup can cause the breaker to trip, especially when the induction cooktop is used alongside other appliances on the same circuit. It may also lead to weak or inconsistent heating if the voltage supply is insufficient. Additionally, incorrect or substandard wiring can pose serious safety risks, including potential electrical hazards.
